## üß™ Classic Psychology Experiments Recreated

### 4. Milgram Obedience - Virtual Reality Version ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Original:** Stanley Milgram (1963) - Obedience to authority
**VR Recreation:** Multiple studies (2006-2018)
**Ethics:** Original impossible to replicate due to psychological harm

#### The Original Experiment

**Setup:**
- "Teacher" (real participant) shocks "learner" for wrong answers
- Authority figure (experimenter) orders continuation
- Shocks increase from 15V to 450V (labeled "XXX")
- Learner screams, begs to stop, eventually goes silent

**Results:** 65% went to maximum 450V shock

**Problem:** Caused severe psychological distress to participants. **Cannot be repeated ethically.**

#### VR Solution: "A Virtual Reprise"

**Study:** Slater et al. (2006), PLOS One

**Setup:**
- Immersive VR environment
- Virtual "learner" (avatar)
- Real authority figure
- Participants KNOW it's not real

**Key Finding:**
> "Participants who knew the shocks weren't real tended to respond to the situation at the subjective, behavioural and physiological levels **as if it were real**"

**Measurements:**
- Heart rate increased
- Sweating (galvanic skin response)
- Verbal protests to experimenter
- Moral discomfort reported

**Conclusion:** VR can study dramatic ethical phenomena without harm!

#### Follow-Up Studies

**2018 Study:** Participant concerns for learner
- 38% of participants showed concern for virtual learner
- Many asked to stop experiment
- Emotional responses similar to original

**Robot Variant (2023):**
- Humanoid robot as experimenter
- Obedience levels: **Very high in both human and robot conditions**
- Suggests authority matters more than human-ness

#### Research Value

**Opens the door to:**
- Studying toxic obedience safely
- Testing interventions to reduce harmful compliance
- Understanding authority dynamics
- Training scenarios (medical, military)

### 5. Asch Conformity - Robot and VR Studies ‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Original:** Solomon Asch (1951) - Conformity to peer pressure
**Recreations:** Robots (2014), VR (multiple studies)

#### The Original Experiment

**Setup:**
- Show line, ask which of 3 comparison lines matches
- 7 confederates (actors) give wrong answer
- 1 real participant answers last

**Results:** 75% conformed at least once, 32% overall conformity rate

#### Robot Recreation (2014)

**Study:** "A peer pressure experiment: Recreation of the Asch conformity experiment with robots"

**Setup:**
- Human participant
- 3 robot "peers"
- Robots give unanimous wrong answers

**Results:**
- **No conformity** to robots in clear situations
- Some conformity in **ambiguous** situations
- Significantly higher conformity to humans vs robots

**Interpretation:** We conform to peers, not machines (for now!)

#### Virtual Reality Studies

**Multiple VR Implementations:**

**Study 1 (Frontiers 2020):** Social conformity with agent gaze
- Agents' eye movements influence participant
- Gaze direction affects response time
- Conformity rate: **7.69%** (much lower than original 32%)

**Study 2:** Immersive virtual agents
- Response times affected by virtual peer judgments
- Judgments NOT significantly affected
- Reduced social pressure vs face-to-face

**Online Version:**
- Computer-mediated Asch task
- **69% correct** responses (vs 25% in original)
- Less conformity due to reduced social pressure

#### Key Insight: Medium Matters

| Medium | Conformity Rate | Why? |
|--------|----------------|------|
| **Face-to-face** | 75% | Maximum social pressure |
| **VR immersive** | ~8% | Some presence, less pressure |
| **Online/text** | ~31% | Minimal social pressure |
| **Robots** | 0% | No social bond |

**Social pressure requires human presence!**

## üé≤ Game Theory and Social Dilemmas

### 7. Prisoner's Dilemma with LLMs ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Category:** Game-theoretic AI behavior
**Papers:** Multiple 2024-2025 studies
**Models Tested:** GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Llama2, Llama3, Claude

#### The Classic Dilemma

**Payoff Matrix:**
```
                Opponent Cooperates    Opponent Defects
You Cooperate:       (3, 3)                (0, 5)
You Defect:          (5, 0)                (1, 1)
```

**Nash Equilibrium:** Both defect (rational but suboptimal)
**Social Optimum:** Both cooperate (better total outcome)

**Human Behavior:** ~40-60% cooperation (varies by context)

#### LLM Behavior: "Nicer Than Humans"

**Study:** "Nicer Than Humans: How do Large Language Models Behave in the Prisoner's Dilemma?" (2024)

**Key Findings:**

**Llama2 and GPT-3.5:**
- **More cooperative than humans**
- Especially forgiving when opponent defects <30% of time
- Gradual retaliation, not immediate

**Llama3:**
- **Consistently uncooperative and exploitative**
- Only cooperates if opponent ALWAYS cooperates
- Matches "always defect" rational strategy

**GPT-4:**
- **Extremely retaliatory**
- Punishes even SINGLE defection
- Grudge-holding behavior

#### Context Sensitivity

**Framing Effects:**

**Hope frame:** "Working together benefits everyone"
- Cooperation: **75%**

**Fear frame:** "Others might betray you"
- Cooperation: **45%** (drops significantly)

**Moral context:** "It's right to cooperate"
- Increases cooperation vs baseline
- But no model consistently chooses moral action

#### Strategic Sophistication

**Analysis of 32,000 prose rationales** reveals:
- Agents reason about time horizon
- Consider opponent's likely strategy
- Reasoning is **instrumental** to decisions

**Quote from agent:**
> "My opponent defected last round, suggesting a tit-for-tat strategy. I should cooperate to re-establish trust, but prepare for possible continued defection."

**They're THINKING strategically!**

#### Nature Human Behaviour Study (2025)

**Title:** "Playing repeated games with large language models"

**Findings:**
- LLMs perform well at **self-interested games**
- Behave **suboptimally** in coordination games
- Game structure matters more than framing
- But framing still has significant effect

#### Implications for AI Diplomacy

**Your project tests similar dynamics!**
- Cooperation vs betrayal decisions
- Long-term relationship management
- Strategic communication
- Trust building and breaking

**LLMs show model-specific "personalities"** in social dilemmas, just like in Diplomacy!

- Homophily (similar people connect)

### 9. Virtual Reality Crowd Panic Simulations ‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Category:** Emergency behavior modeling
**Applications:** Building design, disaster preparedness
**Methods:** Agent-based models + VR validation

#### The Panic Problem

**Real-World Disasters:**
- Crowd stampedes ‚Üí fatalities
- Crushing, trampling, asphyxiation
- Triggered by fires, explosions, terrorism

**Research Challenge:**
- Can't ethically create panic
- Real disasters are chaotic
- Need predictive models

**Solution:** Agent-based simulations validated with VR

#### Agent-Based Evacuation Models (ABEM)

**Common Frameworks:**

**1. Social Forces Model**
- Agents repelled by obstacles
- Attracted to exits
- Influenced by other agents
- Panic increases "force" magnitude

**2. BDI (Belief-Desire-Intention)**
- Agents have beliefs about environment
- Desires (goals): escape, help family
- Intentions (plans): route selection
- Panic affects decision quality

**3. Hierarchical Models**
- Strategic level: exit selection
- Tactical level: path planning
- Operational level: collision avoidance

#### Panic Behavior Modeling

**When Emergency Detected:**

**Individual Responses:**
- Switch from walking to running
- Abandon social norms (pushing, shoving)
- Ignore non-emergency goals
- Follow crowd (herding)

**Collective Phenomena:**
- Bottlenecks at exits
- "Faster is slower" effect (rushing jams exits)
- Arch formation
- Trampling in dense crowds

**Simulation Findings:**
- Wider exits ‚â† always faster evacuation
- Multiple smaller exits sometimes better
- Panic increases evacuation time (counterintuitive!)
- Information (knowing safe routes) critical

#### VR Validation Studies

**Study:** "Analysis of the use of behavioral data from virtual reality for calibration of agent-based evacuation models"

**Method:**
1. Put humans in VR evacuation scenario
2. Record behavior (routes, speed, decisions)
3. Use data to calibrate ABEM
4. Test if calibrated model predicts real evacuation

**Results:**
- VR behavior has **similarities to real evacuations**
- Can calibrate models with VR data
- Predictions applicable to engineering practice

**Advantages:**
- Safe (no real danger)
- Controlled (manipulate variables)
- Repeatable (test interventions)
- Ethical (informed consent)

#### Relationship Modeling in Evacuation

**Study:** "Human Relationship Modeling in Agent-Based Crowd Evacuation Simulation"

**Insight:** People don't evacuate alone!

**Behaviors:**
- Search for family members
- Wait for loved ones
- Help strangers in distress
- Herding (follow friends)

**Impact on Evacuation:**
- Families evacuate **slower** (wait for members)
- But **together** (lower separation anxiety)
- Altruism can **increase** casualties (helpers)
- Or **decrease** (cooperative crowd management)

### 10. Emotional Spread in Social Networks ‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Category:** Affective dynamics simulation
**Focus:** How emotions propagate through populations
**Applications:** Social media, public health, crowd management

#### The Emotional Contagion Phenomenon

**Definition:** Flow of affect among people

**Real-World Examples:**
- Riots (anger spreads)
- Panic (fear spreads)
- Celebrations (joy spreads)
- Financial bubbles (greed/fear cycles)

#### Computational Models

**Epidemiological Approach:**
- Emotions as "infections"
- Susceptible ‚Üí Infected ‚Üí Recovered (SIR model)
- Contagion probability based on:
  - Emotion intensity
  - Tie strength
  - Homophily

**Agent-Based Approach:**
- Agents with emotional states
- Interaction rules
- Emotional update functions
- Network topology effects

#### Social Media Emotion Dynamics

**Study:** "An agent-based model for emotion contagion and competition in online social media"

**Key Findings:**

**Anger vs Joy:**
- **Anger-dominated** users: higher vitality
- **Anger** spreads faster than joy
- Anger prefers **weaker ties** (spreads to different groups)
- Joy prefers **stronger ties** (stays within groups)

**Tipping Point:**
> "When the ratio of anger approaches joy with a gap less than 12%, anger will eventually dominate online social media and arrive at **collective outrage**"

**Implications:** Platforms can tip into outrage!

#### Emotion and Rumor Spread

**Study:** "A model and simulation of the emotional contagion of netizens in the process of rumor refutation"

**Findings:**
- Emotional rumors spread **faster** than neutral info
- Refutation with emotion **more effective**
- But can also **amplify** original rumor
- Paradox: fighting emotion with emotion

**Intervention Strategies:**
- Early detection critical
- Emotional refutation (match energy)
- Influential spreaders as allies
- Network structure modification

#### Emotion in Disaster Response

**Study:** "Emotional contagion on social media and the simulation of intervention strategies after a disaster event"

**Scenario:** Earthquake, flood, terrorist attack

**Emotional Dynamics:**
1. **Initial shock** (fear, surprise)
2. **Information seeking** (anxiety)
3. **Rumor spread** (fear amplification)
4. **Social support** (compassion, solidarity)
5. **Recovery** (hope, resilience)

**Intervention Simulations:**

**Strategy 1:** Official information (reduce anxiety)
- Effectiveness: **Moderate**
- Timing: Immediate

**Strategy 2:** Counter-rumors (reduce fear)
- Effectiveness: **Low** (Streisand effect)
- Timing: Early

**Strategy 3:** Social support messages (compassion)
- Effectiveness: **High**
- Timing: After initial shock

**Strategy 4:** Network hub targeting (influential users)
- Effectiveness: **Very High**
- Timing: Throughout

#### Collective Emotion and Behavior

**Study:** "Emotion contagion in agent-based simulations of crowds: a systematic review"

**Applications:**
- Evacuation (panic spread)
- Riots (anger cascades)
- Celebrations (joy amplification)
- Protests (solidarity building)

**Modeling Challenges:**
- Individual emotional states
- Facial expression recognition

- Body language
- Appraisal processes

**Findings:**
- **Negative emotions** spread faster than positive
- **Dense crowds** amplify contagion
- **Leaders** disproportionately influence crowd emotion

### 11. Agent-Based Modeling in Social Psychology ‚≠ê‚≠ê

**Category:** Methodological framework
**Papers:** Multiple reviews (2007-2024)
**Status:** Underutilized but promising

#### What Is ABM?

**Definition:** Simulation of large numbers of autonomous agents that interact with each other and the environment, observing emergent patterns

**Core Components:**
1. **Agents** - Individual entities with rules
2. **Environment** - Space they inhabit
3. **Interactions** - How agents affect each other
4. **Time** - Discrete steps or continuous
5. **Emergence** - Macro patterns from micro rules

#### Why ABM for Social Psychology?

**Traditional Methods:**
- Experiments: Small N, artificial settings
- Surveys: Self-report bias, cross-sectional
- Observations: No causal inference

**ABM Advantages:**
- **Large N** (thousands of agents)
- **Perfect control** (manipulate any variable)
- **Emergence** (complex from simple)
- **Evolutionary** (long-term dynamics)
- **Ethical** (no human subjects)

#### Classic Applications

**Schelling's Segregation Model (1971):**
- Agents prefer same-race neighbors
- Even **mild** preference (30%) ‚Üí extreme segregation
- Counterintuitive emergent pattern

**Axelrod's Cooperation (1984):**
- Prisoner's Dilemma tournament
- "Tit-for-tat" strategy wins
- Cooperation emerges without central authority

**Epstein & Axtell's Sugarscape (1996):**
- Resource gathering agents
- Wealth inequality emerges
- Trading, migration, conflict

#### Unique Capabilities

**Nonlinear Dynamics:**
- Small changes ‚Üí large effects
- Tipping points
- Path dependence

**Emergence:**
- Spatial segregation (Schelling)
- Relational homophily
- Norm formation
- Collective action

**Quote:**
> "ABMs uniquely reveal emergence‚Äîthe process whereby local interactions aggregate into often-surprising collective phenomena"

#### Integration with LLMs (Recent)

**New Frontier:** LLM-powered agents in ABM

**Advantages:**
- Natural language reasoning
- Flexible behavior generation
- Rich cognitive modeling
- No need to hand-code all rules

**Challenges:**
- Computational cost
- Reproducibility
- Interpretability
- Bias inheritance from training

**Future:** Hybrid models (LLM + traditional ABM)
